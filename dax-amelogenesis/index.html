About Dax Slate Amelogenesis Imperfecta Foundation

A child should not be embarrassed to smile. A child should not fear ridicule or fear being ostracized for being born with Amelogenesis Imperfecta. Even more important, a child should be able to eat an apple without being in pain. For a child with AI, eating is a difficult and often painful experience because they do not have enamel on their teeth.

With your help, we are working to change that. Until there is a cure, at least we can give smiles back to children and make eating a normal activity while warding off future medical complications.

Donate Now

The Dax Slate Amelogenesis Foundation was born out of a selfless act. In a moment of clarity and commitment, Dax Slate offered my husband, Don, his kidney. Don’s kidneys were failing due to a genetic disorder that took the lives of both his mother and grandmother. Although Dax Slate barely knew Don, he stepped in to save his life. So, too, his girlfriend and caregiver selflessly jumped in to travel far from her children to take care of Dax so that he could donate his kidney. It was a story of one selfless act after another. How could my husband and I repay this generosity?

After spending a long day with Dax's girlfriend during the kidney surgeries, I discovered how we could pay it forward. Her 8 year old daughter has AI; she was born with little to no enamel on her teeth. In addition to enduring physical pain when she eats or drinks or when she goes to the dentist, she is teased mercilessly by other children. Both Don and I knew we had to help this child, and children like her, to have a normal childhood, free from taunts from the other children, free from pain when she eats and free from continuing medical issues as a result of having this disease. The effects of this disease are not just embarrassment and discomfort, they can manifest in other lifelong medical complications that can be significantly reduced if we can help these children. Please join us on this journey to aid as many children as possible who otherwise cannot afford treatment.

--Cara Looper, Director

About Amelogenesis Imperfecta

Amelogenesis imperfecta refers to a group of rare, inherited disorders characterized by abnormal enamel formation. The term is typically restricted to those disorders of enamel development not associated with other abnormalities of the body.

Clinical researchers usually classify AI into four main types of which 17 subtypes are recognized. The main types are based on clinical appearance, radiographic appearance and enamel thickness, and the subtypes are based on mode of inheritance and gene mutation. The main types are: hypoplastic (type I); hypomaturation (type II); hypocalcified (type III); and hypomaturation/hypoplasia/taurodontism (type IV). AI may be inherited as an X-linked, autosomal dominant, or autosomal recessive genetic trait, depending on the subtype.

Affected Populations

AI affects apporoximately 1 out of 14,000 to 16,000 children in the United States. Of this number, about 40% have the hypocalcified dominant type. The autosomal dominant and recessive forms of the disorder affect males and females in equal numbers. The X-linked dominant type of the disorder affects twice as many males as females. The X-linked recessive type affects only males.

Signs & Symptoms

AI is characterized by defective or missing tooth enamel.

Secondary effects of this disorder may be cracked teeth, early tooth decay and/or loss, in addition to susceptibility to multiple diseases of the tissues surrounding teeth (periodontal tissues) such as gums, cementum, ligaments, and alveolar bones in which the tooth root rests. Teeth are also sensitive to both hot or cold exposures, and sometimes both.

Treatment

Full crown restorations and a type of denture that caps defective teeth and corrects open bite are excellent treatments for this disorder. Desensitizing toothpaste can prevent painful sensitivity to heat and cold. Good oral hygiene is important. Genetic counseling is recommending for families of children with AI.
